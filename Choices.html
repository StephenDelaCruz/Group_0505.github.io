Government Surveillance in New Zealand



Choices and Opportunites



In the area of Law and order, war and peace, the government have made advances to keep the population in check with the use of surveillance. Furthermore, the use of surveillance is highly manipulated when the government or population feel vulnerable and threatened (Baecker, 2019). This results in the government monitoring everything and everyone with the use of digital technologies, therefore, minimising hostile acts occurring. This is just one example of the choices the government makes when deciding what to how to monitor the citizens and keep them ‘safe’.



As we know, acts of terrorism play a factor to making the government and citizens feel threatened and vulnerable. In 2017 on March the 22nd, a man by the name of Khalid Masood, had murdered three civilians and a police officer in London. However, prior to the event occurring Masood had been using a messaging app known as WhatsApp, which would encrypt messages that are sent. The British Home Secretary opposed this feature of the app and deemed it “completely unacceptable” (Cooper, S. 2018). Threats like this have opportunities to be avoided if surveillance had been precise. The Government have been reviewing surveillance in countries like, the United Kingdom, the United States, and Australia since 2013 (Cooper, S. 2018). The Government have already made their choice to improve and work on the state of their surveillance in their country. Similarly in New Zealand, when our country was shocked by one of the worst acts of terrorism. The 2019 Christchurch Mosque attack. For the following months after the shooting, several articles were published about the fear of other attacks occurring and discussed how the security of New Zealand needed to be intensified. After the occurrence of this attack for the first time in the history of New Zealand, the set the terrorism threat level to ‘high’ (Every-Palmer, Susanna, et al. 2021). This choice was made by the New Zealand Security Intelligence Service and demanded that guards and armed policemen (New Zealand police are rarely armed) are to be stationed at important places nationwide, including places like hospitals, churches, and mosques etc to keep surveillance over the designated areas.



We are currently in the year 2023 and the world we live in today, everything revolves around the internet and the worldwide web. Without the internet we wouldn’t be able to access anything, whether it be to make an important email to someone, or to search up a tutorial on YouTube, or even to check your status on social media. The internet holds so much information for us to see. However, what we search on the internet, the government know abouts. A study by Alex Marthews and Catherine Tucker (2017), states that “The Surveillance revelations are treated as an exogenous shock in information about how closely users’ internet searches were being monitored by the US government” (p.1). Although this is not in New Zealand specifically, it still goes to show the extents the government would choose to go to keep an eye on the civilians, through telecommunication interceptions. This sort of surveillance may not be that bad to us here in New Zealand, but it still occurs especially for migrants. As migrants are away from friends and family, online interaction becomes their only way of contacting their loved ones, meaning they would spend heaps of time over the internet (Mohamed, A 2021). Due to the high internet usage by non-citizens and even residents, the government have gained more legislative authority to keep an eye on us using the internet (Mohamed, A. 2021). The technique that the government uses is monitoring and obtaining personal information from the ISP (Internet Service Provider) (Brown, I & Korff, D. 2004). This is quite a scary thing to think about, but unfortunately, it is what the government have decided to do. The government try to uphold this breach of privacy saying that it is a combat against terrorism (Brown, I. & Korff, D. 2004). However, government have also sent guards and armed police to combat against terrorism (Every-Palmer, Susanna, et al. 2021), therefore their argument and choice of using internet search and online interaction for combat against terrorism seems suspicious.



When speaking about government surveillance, everyone has heard about the ‘Snowden leaks’ or also known as the ‘Snowden files’. The Snowden leaks disclosed highly classified information on the NSA’s (National Security Agency) way of surveillance. This brought attention to multiple countries in the world, however, public responses were substantially muted. The Snowden leaks gave the world insight on the techniques used by the NSA to keep track of the citizens. These techniques included, daily communication monitoring, intercepting and data collecting of online usage (Dencik, L. & Cable, J. 2017). This may sound like it may not influence us here in New Zealand, however, you would be wrong. New Zealand is apart of an alliance called Five Eyes and have chosen to be apart of the alliance since 1941. This alliance is made up of five countries, United States of America, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, and of course, New Zealand. This alliance was formed to share intelligence of surveillance to prevent worldwide threats (Pfluke, C. 2019). Therefore, New Zealand have chosen to keep mass surveillance over us for many years now. Our former prime minister, John Key tried to deny the fact that New Zealand didn’t engage in mass surveillance (Snowden, E. 2014), but was shutdown by Kim Dotcom (a political activist and internet entrepreneur) and Snowden. Snowden would go onto say that if the prime minister or anyone with authority states that New Zealand does not engage in any mass surveillance of some sort, they would be false (­­Kuehn, K. 2016). Snowden then states (Snowden, E. 2014) that “If you live in New Zealand, you are being watched.”(p.1 – p.2). As New Zealanders this may sound scary, and rude as the government can see exactly what we are doing anytime of the day, and honestly it is and it is a breach of privacy. But this choice also could prevent us from having another attack of some sort, or anything that could harm us citizens.



In 2019, the world was hit with a pandemic that would initiate a worldwide lockdown that would last around three months. This meant that we all had to stay at home and isolate with our families. If we ever needed to leave the house how would the government be able to keep track of us, to make sure we weren’t doing anything reckless? That’s why the government made us use the COVID tracer app, where we would have to scan the QR code wherever we went (Yang, F. & Heemsbergen, L. 2021). This was a form of surveillance and was used to keep us all safe. If you were to scan in then get covid, the government and health services would retrace your steps (if you were scanning everywhere you went) and find all the spots that could have been contaminated with covid-19. The government took the opportunity to keep everyone safe by initiating the lockdown, then chose to release the covid tracer app to keep surveillance over us and to furthermore keep us safe from the dangers of the highly contagious pandemic or covid-19.
