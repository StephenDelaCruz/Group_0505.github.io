Government Surveillance in New Zealand



Conclusion



Government surveillance is a contentious topic in society and will continue to be for the foreseeable future. The justification for the government to surveil its citizen’s has been largely the same over its history, keeping the citizen’s safe. However it has looked drastically different over time, and across countries. China has an approach that is considered nuclear in other countries controlling what is said, what is published, and even going so far as to rate citazens to decide who gets access to certain privileges (Canales & Mok, 2022). Whereas New Zealand has a comparatively much more relaxed system, the same intention remains, to keep citazens safe, however the questionability of it still remains, is it right for a government to have access to an individuals private, files, conversations or images with the intention of only ever seeing a small percent that belong to bad actors.


New Zealand’s governments form of surveillance isn’t as widely condemned especially by the international community, especially when compared to China. This can ultimately due to the way the New Zealand government governs itself, with regards to creating laws that it then hold itself accountable to, along with ensuring any international agents acting within the country also abide by the laws that have been set, this gives the citazens of New Zealand a sense that the power is not being abused. This was exemplified by the Kim Dotcom case, where agencies not associated with the NZ government, were surveiling a resident of New Zealand illegally, resulting in the case falling a part due to the evidence provided being considered tainted (Radio New Zealand, 2017).


Contrary to this positive act by the government of maintaining a moral high ground while letting a potential criminal get away with a crime. There are instances where a government actor doesn’t necessarily, maintain its morality and the intention behind laws that it creates, such is the case of Snowden where the reality of what data and techniques were being used by the government actor the NSA were leaked, leading to controversy surrounding the privacy of not only U.S citazens but also citazens around the world (Mohamed, A 2021).


There are still many questions yet to be answered, How far is too far for a government to pry in order to save lives? How much does the individual care about their privacy? Should it be necessary to take steps to have privacy, and not just have it assumed? All of which are important to the question is government surveillance good or bad, and how far is too far when it comes to compromising privacy to the individual?
